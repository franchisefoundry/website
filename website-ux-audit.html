Outstanding copy. Conversion architecture needs work.
The single biggest issue: the website and portal tell different stories
The website promises "AI-Powered Franchise Matching" and says "your matches are ready" before the user has done anything. The portal delivers a consultant-led, human-guided process where a match can take days or weeks. This expectation gap is the most likely cause of post-click drop-off. Users click "Find Your Match", land in the portal questionnaire expecting instant AI results, and hit a friction wall when they realise they're creating an account and waiting for a human to review their profile.

The fix isn't complicated — it's about aligning the language. The portal experience is actually better than AI-only: it's expert-matched, personally guided, and free. Lead with that.
Top 9 Priorities — ordered by conversion impact
1
Remove "AI-Powered" from the hero eyebrow
The portal is consultant-led, not AI-automated. Users who click expecting instant AI matching land in a human onboarding process and feel misled. Change to "Expert-Led Franchise Matching" or "Data-Matched. Human-Guided." — both are more accurate and actually more reassuring for a high-stakes financial decision.
High impact · 5 mins
2
Explain what happens after clicking "Find Your Match"
The website never tells users that clicking "Find Your Match" takes them to create a portal account and complete a questionnaire — after which a consultant reviews their profile and gets in touch within [X] days. This unexplained journey is where drop-off happens. Add a "What to expect" note below the hero CTA: "You'll complete a 2-minute questionnaire and create a free account. A consultant will review your profile and be in touch within 1 business day."
High impact · 1 hour
3
Add social proof — the site has none
No testimonials, no success numbers, no "X franchises matched" stats, no logos with "As seen in." For a service asking users to trust you with a £100k–£300k financial decision, this is a critical trust gap. Even 2–3 short quotes from satisfied franchisees, placed just below the hero, would substantially increase conversion. If you don't have quotes yet, lead with credibility stats: "20+ franchise brands matched" or "100% of clients pay nothing."
High impact · 2 hours
4
Strengthen the hero headline
"Finding Your Perfect Franchise Fit" is the most generic possible franchise headline — it reads identically to every broker and portal in the market. Your real differentiator is that you've sat on the franchisor side of the table and you work exclusively in hospitality. Lead with that. Options: "The Franchise Match Built by Insiders" · "We've Sat Across From the Franchisors. Now We're On Your Side." · "Finally, Franchise Advice That Actually Works For You."
High impact · 30 mins
5
Show the hero stats on mobile
The hero stat card (15+ years, Free, Both sides) is hidden on mobile with display:none. These are your three most persuasive trust signals and mobile users — who are most likely to bounce — never see them. Move them below the CTA buttons as a compact inline row instead of the full desktop card. Mobile conversion depends on these trust signals being visible.
High impact · 1 hour
6
Fix the match preview investment ranges
The blurred match preview cards show "£10k – £25k" for a QSR and "£75k – £110k" for Japanese cuisine. These numbers don't match the reality of hospitality franchise investment (typically £80k–£300k). Users who've done any research will immediately notice this is illustrative fiction. Use plausible ranges that match the real brands in your network.
High impact · 10 mins
7
Add in-article CTAs to every blog post
The four article pages have one CTA box each, at the very bottom. Articles about royalty costs and franchisee mistakes are read by people who are actively considering franchising — they are your highest-intent audience. Each article should have 2–3 embedded CTAs: one after the intro, one mid-article, one at the end. The mid-article CTA should be contextually relevant to that article's topic.
Medium impact · 2 hours
8
Add active state indicators to the navigation
When you're on the About page, no nav link shows as active — even though the HTML adds a class="active" to the correct link. The CSS defines styles for .active but without a clear visual indicator like an underline or brighter text, users can't orient themselves. Add a visible bottom underline or white dot indicator for the current page.
Medium impact · 20 mins
9
Consolidate the duplicate footer columns
The footer has a "Quick Links" column and a "Company" column. Both contain About Us, For Franchise Brands, and Blog. This is pure duplication — it looks incomplete and wastes valuable footer space that could carry a newsletter signup or a trust statement.
Medium impact · 15 mins

Website → Portal Journey

This is the most important UX issue on the entire site. A visitor who clicks "Find Your Match" enters a journey the website has not prepared them for. Understanding this gap is critical — it explains most of the post-click drop-off.

4 issues
⚠ The expectation gap
The website says: "AI-Powered" · "Your matches are ready" · "Our matching engine does the work"
The portal delivers: Create an account → complete a questionnaire → wait for a consultant to review your profile → consultant contacts you → get your match revealed in a call

These are fundamentally different experiences. A visitor expecting instant AI results will encounter a registration form, a questionnaire, and then silence — with no indication of how long they'll wait or what happens next. The portal is actually a better product than "AI" would be for this decision. Lead with that.

Content & Messaging

The writing on this website is genuinely excellent — distinctive, honest, and differentiated. The issues are structural: where that copy lives on the page and what's missing entirely.

4 issues
!
No social proof anywhere on the site. The about page mentions "Ben and Harry" but has no photos, no LinkedIn links, and the experience section uses anonymised brand names ("17-Site Burger Chain", "International Franchise Concept"). These are compelling credentials but anonymised examples are less persuasive than named ones. At minimum, named team photos with brief bios would dramatically increase trust.
~
"Why Choose Us" section is a text wall. The green section on the homepage is three paragraphs of dense prose on a #5f725f background. The copy is excellent but it's not scannable. Users skip long text blocks. Break it into 3 short benefit cards with icons, or use a stat + quote combination that's easier to consume at pace.
~
The "For Brands" page doesn't mention the portal. Franchisors who visit for-brands.html and want to sign up have no clear conversion path. There's a partnership CTA but no mention of how the portal works for them — questionnaire, brand profile, match pipeline, candidate dashboard. A section on "what you get access to" would substantially improve franchisor lead quality.
✓
No FAQ page. For a service where users are making a potentially £200k+ financial decision, there are obvious unanswered questions: "How are you paid?", "Are you regulated?", "What types of franchise do you cover?", "How long does the process take?", "What if I don't like my matches?". An FAQ page would both convert hesitant visitors and improve SEO.
✓ What's working well — keep and amplify
The copywriting is genuinely strong. "Not every franchise is the right franchise" — the pullquote — is memorable. "We only partner with brands we'd back ourselves" is differentiated. The article writing is exceptional — real, honest and clearly expert. These are worth amplifying: pull the best lines into the hero and social proof sections rather than burying them in body text lower on the page.

Navigation & CTAs

The nav is clean and functional. Three specific issues reduce its effectiveness as a conversion tool.

3 issues
~
Active page indicator is invisible. The HTML correctly adds class="active" to nav links. The CSS defines .nav-links a.active { font-weight: 600 } — but the difference between 500 and 600 weight is imperceptible at that font size. Add an underline or bottom border for the active state so users can orient themselves.
~
"Portal Login" is de-prioritised. The login link uses a subtle border style — visually the weakest element in the nav. But returning users (people who have accounts and are mid-journey) are arguably your highest-value traffic. Consider making it slightly more prominent: a solid light background on hover, or a different icon treatment.
✓
"For Franchise Brands" uses gold text which makes it pop — but it also reads slightly like an ad on your own nav (a different audience signal within your nav). This is intentional but worth monitoring. If it causes confusion about whose site this is, a subtle separator or badge might be cleaner.

Blog & Articles

3 issues
!
Articles have only one CTA, at the very bottom. Someone reading "What your royalty is actually costing you each month" is actively considering franchising. By the time they reach the end of a 1,500-word article they've already formed a view. A CTA placed mid-article ("Want us to run these numbers for your specific match? It's free →") converts at dramatically higher rates than a footer box.
~
Article pages have no related articles, no author box, and no share mechanism. Each article is a dead end. A "You might also like" section at the bottom drives session depth, and an author credit (Harry or Ben, with a photo) reinforces that this is expert-written content, not generic SEO copy.
✓
Blog listing page shows only 4 articles with limited preview content. The featured article format is good but the grid below it would benefit from category tags (Unit Economics, Choosing a Brand, Legal, etc.) to help readers find what's most relevant to their stage.

Mobile Experience

The mobile nav overlay is well-executed. The main issues are content that's hidden on mobile and the missed opportunity to surface your strongest conversion assets to mobile users.

3 issues
!
The hero stat card is hidden on mobile (display:none). "15+ years", "Free", "Both sides" — these are your three most persuasive trust signals. On desktop they sit in a visible card. On mobile they're invisible. Given mobile users have a higher bounce rate and shorter attention span, these trust signals are even more important on mobile than on desktop. Move them inline, below the CTA, as compact pills.
~
Mobile CTA in the hamburger menu points to the questionnaire. The mobile menu CTA "Find Your Match →" uses a gold button that takes users to the portal. This is correct. But users who've already signed up have to scroll past all the nav links to find the "Portal Login" option. Consider reordering the mobile menu to put Portal Login closer to the top for returning users.
✓
The geometric hero panel (::before pseudo-element) is already correctly hidden on mobile. This is well done. The match preview browser mockup also collapses gracefully. The main mobile issue is purely the stat card visibility.

Footer & Technical Structure

The footer is functional but has two clear problems: duplicated columns and missing information. The technical structure (inline CSS per page) is a maintainability issue that's not a UX problem today but will slow down future changes.

3 issues
~
Duplicate "Quick Links" and "Company" columns. Both contain About Us, For Franchise Brands, and Blog. This looks unfinished and wastes 25% of the footer's horizontal space. Merge them into one column and use the freed space for a newsletter signup or a trust statement.
~
Each HTML page has its own full CSS block, including several layers of mobile overrides. The homepage CSS has comments like "MOBILE REDESIGN v3" — a sign of iterative patching. This isn't a user-facing problem now, but it means any future design change must be made in 6+ files. Consider a shared stylesheet for the common nav/footer/button styles.
✓
No phone number in the footer. The contact section only shows the email address. For a high-trust service, showing a phone number (even a mobile) signals accessibility and confidence. If you don't want calls, a calendar booking link (Calendly/TidyCal) would achieve the same effect.
